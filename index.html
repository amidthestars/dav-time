Lecture Plan

Lecture 1: Introduction

  • Introduction to the project
  • This is the FPGA project, why FPGAs?
  • General purpose CPU vs FPGA vs ASIC
  • How do we program them? It's Verilog time
  • Introduction to Verilog - modules, wires, bitwise review
  • Introduction to our FPGA in particular

Lecture 2: Combinational Logic + More Verilog

  • Overview of digital design and why it's useful
  • Review of gates and boolean expressions
  • Always blocks and reg's
  • Multiplexers and what they look like in Verilog
  • Other iterative statements (for, while loops)
  • Basics of testbenching + sample use cases

Lecture 3: Sequential Logic

  • Why sequential? aka. why Verilog is Different
  • Clocks and clock dividing
  • Sensitivity lists and posedge / negedge
  • Building off the above, latches and why they should be canceled
  • Blocking versus nonblocking
  • Finite state machines and what they look like in Verilog

Lecture 4: VGA

  • What is VGA
  • Frame buffers
  • Ping pong memory

Lecture 5: Good Verilog practices + CS 33 Review

  • Parameters, name things well, make sure you always have defined behavior
  • PLLs as clock dividers
  • Logic analyzer and how to use it
  • RAM, BRAM and how to use them in Verilog

Track separation time!

Lecture 6: Serial Communication

  • What is serial communication?
  • what makes a good protocol + examples of protocols
  • Thorough walkthrough of I2C

Lecture 7: Fast Fourier Transforms

  • Fourier transforms and sampling
  • Fast fourier transforms
  • Butterfly unit and twiddle factors

Lab Plan

Lab 1: Setup + Intro to Verilog

Learning goals:

  • Getting used to Verilog syntax
  • Bitwise operations, either decryption/encryption
  • Ideally, getting used to IDE

Lab 2: Working with the FPGA

Learning goals:

  • The joy that is pin planning
  • Manipulating FPGA parts with Verilog
  • Button debouncing
  • Getting inputs from FPGA

Lab 3: Finite state machines and sequential logic

Learning goals:

  • Finite state machines are pretty important
  • Sequential logic triggered on conditions

Lab 4: VGA

Learning goals:

  • Get FPGA to communicate with VGA
  • Display graphics on VGA that can be manipulated by FPGA

Lab 5: Depending on what track, FFT or I2C

Making the other lab optional to cut down on DAV workload, and get project proposals started earlier

Learning outcomes:

  • FFT: Make FFT processor and figure out butterfly units
  • I2C: Get FPGA to communicate with nunchuk

Proposed Changes

Organization

Emphasizing track separation

  • DAV is already quite a bit of work; allowing students to focus on what they need for capstone will cut down on workload
  • Students are still able to complete the other lab if they wish

More emphasis on verification

(more testbenches)

  • Get students used to testbenching to solve their problems
  • Logic analyzer for verifying communication tests

More visualizations

(publicity moment)

  • When DAV delves into difficult topics, more visualizations will make the content more digestible
  • Don't necessarily want students to have to figure these concepts out while doing labs; ideally, the lecture teaches them and they know what they're doing going into the lab
  • Step by step walkthroughs with drawings or visuals may help this process

Conclusion

DAV already has a strong foundation. The goal is to continue to refine DAV to make it more established and concrete, so that others may be able to use DAV's curriculum.

At the same time, adding to DAV at this point doesn't make too much sense. Assuming future leads (like now) join DAV and complete one of the two tracks, they can learn the other after they become leads. Adding more material would steepen the learning curve, and future leads may find more additions hard to keep.
