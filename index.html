Lecture and Lab Plan

Organization

Fall Quarter

Lecture 1: Introduction (Week 3)

  • Introduction to the project
  • This is the FPGA project, why FPGAs?
  • General purpose CPU vs FPGA vs ASIC
  • How do we program them? It's Verilog time
  • Introduction to Verilog - modules, wires, bitwise review
  • Introduction to our FPGA in particular

Explanation: I'm putting all of the introductory information to the project in the first lecture, to give students a high-level view of what they'll be working with. Verilog will be somewhat covered, with an emphasis on how it's different than other programming languages that students may have seen before, but most of the teaching of Verilog will be done separately.

DAV'ing: Quartus Setup + Questa Reading + Verilog

Quartus was one of the hardest things to set up, so being there in person to help students set it up would be optimal.

Questa also has some quirks that might be helpful to teach in person.

Finally, while Lecture 1 does cover some basic Verilog, which will be enough to help students do the first lab, this worksession / workshop will be a chance for students to ask questions and get a head start on the lab.

Lab 1: Setup + Intro to Verilog (Released after 1st lecture, due Week 5)

Learning goals:

  • Getting used to Verilog syntax
  • Bitwise operations, either decryption/encryption
  • Ideally, getting used to IDE

Lecture 2: Combinational Logic + More Verilog (Week 5)

  • Overview of digital design and why it's useful
  • Review of gates and boolean expressions
  • Always blocks and reg's
  • Multiplexers and what they look like in Verilog
  • Other iterative statements (for, while loops)
  • Basics of testbenching

Explanation: In order to teach students how to write code that is synthesizable, logic elements such as multiplexers will be covered. Information on the basic logic gates will be included in the slides as a recap, but may not be emphasized.

Lab 2: Working with the FPGA (Released after the 2nd lecture; due Week 7)

Learning goals:

  • The joy that is pin planning
  • Manipulating FPGA parts with Verilog
  • Button debouncing
  • Getting inputs from FPGA

Lecture 3: Sequential Logic (Week 7)

  • Why sequential? aka. why Verilog is Different
  • Clocks and clock dividing
  • Sensitivity lists and posedge / negedge
  • Building off the above, latches and why they should be canceled
  • Blocking versus nonblocking
  • Finite state machines and what they look like in Verilog

Explanation: Due to the importance of FSMs, there will be examples of what they look like in Verilog.

Lab 3: Finite state machines and sequential logic (Released after the 3rd lecture; due Week 9)

Learning goals:

  • Finite state machines are pretty important
  • Sequential logic triggered on conditions

DAV'Ing: Testbenching

Showcasing different scenarios and testbenches that would help Working with Questa to debug

Lecture 4: Good Verilog practices + CS 33 Review (Week 9)

  • Parameters, name things well, make sure you always have defined behavior
  • PLLs as clock dividers
  • Logic analyzer and how to use it
  • RAM, BRAM and how to use them in Verilog

Explanation: This lecture isn't accompanied with a lab, and it's a nice way to wrap up the quarter. Fall quarter is mostly about getting the basics down with just the FPGA; peripherals and more capstone related topics will be covered in the next quarter.

Winter Quarter

Lecture 5: VGA (Week 2)

  • What is VGA
  • Frame buffers
  • Ping pong memory

Explanation: This is the last lecture that applies to both tracks, so it will be covered first. From here on out, people in either track can choose to specialize to conserve their time. Project proposals will probably have to start happening around this time, too.

Lab 4: VGA (Released after lecture 4, due Week 4)

Learning goals:

Track separation time!

Deposit Plan / Drop Policy

The deposit will be $50, returned as follows:

  • $10 returned upon completion of fall labs
  • $15 returned upon completion of winter labs
  • $25 returned upon completion of spring labs + capstone

Drop policy

Teams will be dropped after three weeks of not finishing a project and no communication.

In order to get extensions, teams just need to be communicative and show their progress, whether it's in person or with screenshots.

Proposed Changes

Organization

Emphasizing track separation

  • DAV is already quite a bit of work; allowing students to focus on what they need for capstone will cut down on workload
  • Students are still able to complete the other lab if they wish, it's just no longer mandatory
  • After a certain point after the lab deadlines, solutions will be released to help with capstone progress. In terms of the optional lab, only one will need to be completed in order to get the full deposit back.

DAV Returners

Luring the old people back

  • With two tracks, people may want to try the other track
  • Only based off of the final capstone, no labs required
  • Want to see more cool projects :D

More emphasis on verification

(more testbenches)

  • Get students used to testbenching to solve their problems
  • Logic analyzer for verifying communication tests
  • Workshops and main lecture time to cover these topics

More visualizations

(publicity moment)

  • When DAV delves into difficult topics, more visualizations will make the content more digestible
  • Also working towards the goal of making slides easily accessible if the curriculum were to be passed on to other schools
  • Step by step walkthroughs with drawings or visuals may help this process

Conclusion

DAV already has a strong foundation. The goal is to continue to refine DAV to make it more established and concrete, so that others may be able to use DAV's curriculum.

At the same time, adding to DAV at this point doesn't make too much sense. Assuming future leads (like now) join DAV and complete one of the two tracks, they can learn the other after they become leads. Adding more material would steepen the learning curve, and future leads may find more additions hard to keep.
